Self-study tips (Part 1)

Published Monday Jan 13, 2020

Finding it tricky to keep on top of studying for the CAA written exams? We're here to help! Finding time in between everything else going on can be tough and study's often the first thing to get bumped when things get too busy.

This week is the first of a two-parter, with nine tips to help stay motivated, optimise your study time, and get the exams done.

1. Know what you need to know

The first step is to make sure you’re studying the right things and, more importantly, don’t miss anything out. Good news! The CAA provides a list of topics that can be covered in each exam, published in Advisory Circulars (ACs).

ACs are available for free from the CAA website. Different ACs cover different subjects, but the ones we’re after start with “AC61-”, which give the requirements for a specific to a license or rating. For example, AC61-3 is the guide for PPL, AC61-5 for CPL, etc.

These guides contain a lot of other information about the license/rating, so finding the exam syllabus can be a little tricky. Open the AC and search for “Written Examination Syllabus”. This is the main heading for all subjects - scroll down until you find the specific exam you’re studying for.

It’s a good idea to have a quick look through all the topics before you start studying, and then again before you take the exam to make sure you don’t get any surprises.

2. Realistic goals

Sitting the exam in a week after back-to-back marathon study sessions might sound like a good idea, but... realistic? If it is, don't let me get in your way... go for it! But if not, maybe it's a better idea to dial the intensity back and avoid study sessions becoming something that goes straight to the Too Hard Basket.

That said, don’t go too far the other way either. The most important component is consistency - doing at least a little bit regularly - and a schedule that works with your lifestyle. Staying up until 2am studying every night's no good if you burn out after a few days.

Which brings us to...

3. Make a schedule. Stick with it.

You've committed to getting the exam done, now make it a priority. OK it's tough, but stick at it and exam day will come, then it's done. (Well, except for KDRs, but we'll talk about those some other time).

Think honestly about how much time you can set aside for studying, especially if you have other commitments like work, family etc. The important thing is that it doesn't become such a burden that it starts getting ignored in favour of other things. Once you've figured out how much time you can set aside, schedule it in, and do your best not to let anything else get in the way of that time.

4. Short, frequent sessions

Science says shorter and more frequent sessions are more likely to help you retain what you're taking in. Sometimes the long study sessions are unavoidable - but if at all possible aim for regular, relatively brief periods to study. This can be especially powerful if you combine regular revision along with the new material.
